# Leg 2: Grand Canyon
**Joshua Tree → Route 66 → Grand Canyon South Rim → Zion (handoff)**
**Dates:** April 17–19 (2 nights, 2 full days + departure morning)

---

## ⚠️ Key Facts & Watch-Outs

- **No timed entry permit required** at South Rim — just pay the $35 vehicle entrance fee at the gate (no cash accepted; or use an America the Beautiful pass if you have one).
- **Mather Campground** requires advance reservation March–November. **Reserve on recreation.gov — 🔴 do this now.**
- **Desert View Campground** opens April 11, 2026 — will be open for their April 18 stay. Also reserve on recreation.gov.
- **South Kaibab Trail has zero shade** — start by 8am or wait until late afternoon. April temps on the rim are mild (highs ~60–65°F) but the inner canyon gets warm.
- **Hermit Road** is closed to private vehicles spring through fall — free park shuttle runs every 15–30 min from the Village.
- **America the Beautiful Annual Pass** ($80) covers entrance to all national parks. With Grand Canyon + multiple Utah parks ahead, this pays for itself immediately. Buy at the first park entrance gate or online.

## Day 12 — Apr 17: Joshua Tree → Grand Canyon (via Route 66)

**Drive:** ~7.5 hrs with stops (365 miles). Leave by 8am.

### Recommended Route: The Route 66 Option

Instead of the all-highway I-40 slog, take the Route 66 corridor — it adds ~45 minutes but the stops are genuinely worth it:

**Joshua Tree → I-10 East → US-95 North → Needles, CA**
- Needles is a classic desert crossroads. Gas up here.

**Needles → Oatman, AZ (via old Route 66 over the Black Mountains)**
- The section of Route 66 over Sitgreaves Pass into Oatman is dramatic — steep switchbacks through desert mountains, completely unlike the surrounding flatlands.
- **Oatman** is a well-preserved gold mining ghost town. Wild burros (descendants of miners' pack animals) roam freely through the streets and will walk right up to you. The Oatman Hotel is where Clark Gable and Carole Lombard spent their honeymoon. Free museum inside, bar/restaurant.
- Allow 30–45 min here.

**Oatman → Kingman → Hackberry General Store**
- **Hackberry General Store** — the best single photo stop on Arizona Route 66. An explosion of vintage gas pumps, old cars, neon signs, and Route 66 memorabilia. 30 min east of Kingman on Route 66.
- Great backdrop for a trip photo.

**Hackberry → Williams, AZ**
- **Williams** is the official "Gateway to the Grand Canyon" on Route 66. Charming downtown, good lunch options. 1 hour from the South Rim.
- Grab lunch here. **Celiac dining in Williams:**
  - **Nany's Tacos** ✅ CELIAC-AWARE — 324 E Railroad Ave. Best Celiac option in Williams. Corn tortillas throughout, knowledgeable owner, staff understand cross-contamination (not just GF preference). They clean the grill thoroughly for GF orders. Most tacos are GF except two (the red-sauce ones use flour as a thickener — ask). No dedicated fryer so skip chips. Barbacoa tacos are consistently praised. Celiac diners report no reactions.
  - **Red Raven Restaurant** — local favorite for atmosphere but no specific Celiac-safe protocols documented; use caution and ask explicitly about cross-contamination if you go.
  - **Pine Country Restaurant** ✅ GF-FRIENDLY — marks GF items on menu, knowledgeable staff. ⚠️ Hash browns are labeled GF but cooked in a shared fryer — skip fried items.

**Williams → Grand Canyon South Rim**
- ~1 hr north on AZ-64. Arrive mid-to-late afternoon.

## Celiac Dining Strategy for This Leg

Options thin out significantly once you leave the coast. The South Rim is a captive-audience dining situation — know what's safe before you arrive.

- **Stock the van** before leaving LA or Palm Springs. Carry reliable GF provisions through the desert.
- **Say "Celiac disease"** explicitly at every restaurant — especially at Grand Canyon lodge dining where staff turnover is high.
- **El Tovar** is the clear best option on the South Rim — make a reservation.
- **Kanab** is a genuine bright spot between Grand Canyon and Zion — better options than expected for a small town.

## Camping: Two-Campground Strategy

Splitting nights between the two campgrounds gives you the best of both sides of the rim:

### Night 1: Mather Campground
- Largest campground, 1 mile from Canyon Village and trailheads. 327 sites, no hookups.
- Showers and laundry available at the nearby Camper Village (small fee).
- Walk or shuttle to the rim for sunset your first evening.
- 🔴 **Reserve on [recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov)** — books up months in advance for April

### Night 2: Desert View Campground
- 26 miles east of the Village on Desert View Drive — smaller (50 sites), quieter, and right at the east rim.
- Open April 11–Oct 11, 2026. No hookups, no showers — your van is ideal.
- Positioned perfectly for departure to Utah: you're already on the east end of the park, just hop on US-89 north toward Zion.
- 🔴 **Reserve on [recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov)**

## Day 13 — Apr 18: Full Day at the Grand Canyon

### Morning: Sunrise + South Kaibab Trail
**Sunrise at Yavapai Point** — less crowded than Mather Point, faces both east and west. Arrive 30 min before sunrise. The Yavapai Geology Museum next door has panoramic observation windows and is worth a look.

**South Kaibab Trail to Cedar Ridge** ⭐ — best moderate hike on the South Rim.
- 3 miles round trip, ~1,140 ft of descent (and re-ascent)
- Follows an exposed ridgeline with 270° canyon views — dramatically better perspective than the rim trail
- Ooh Aah Point (~0.9 miles in) is a standout viewpoint even if you turn around early
- **No water, no shade** — start by 8am, bring at least 2L per person
- Take the Kaibab/Rim Route shuttle (orange line) to the trailhead — parking there is extremely limited

### Afternoon: Desert View Drive
Drive the 25-mile Desert View Drive east along the rim — this is one of the park's great scenic drives and gets far less traffic than the west (Hermit) side.

Key stops heading east:
- **Grandview Point** — one of the widest panoramas on the South Rim, often overlooked
- **Moran Point** — named after painter Thomas Moran; great canyon depth views
- **Lipan Point** — arguably the best view of the Colorado River from the South Rim, including a bend in the river
- **Desert View Watchtower** ⭐ — Mary Colter's 1932 stone tower at the canyon's east rim. Climb to the top for views extending to the North Rim and beyond. A genuine architectural landmark. Navajo trading post and snack bar at the base.

Move your van to Desert View Campground in the late afternoon.

**Celiac dining on the South Rim:**
- **El Tovar Dining Room** ✅ CELIAC-AWARE — the best dining option in the park. Has a dedicated GF menu; celiac diners report eating safely here ("my celiac daughter and I ate safely for lunch"). The nicest restaurant on the rim — reserve ahead for dinner. Reservations open 6 months in advance at grandcanyonlodges.com.
- **Yavapai Lodge Dining Hall** ✅ GF-FRIENDLY — has a GF menu; positive reports from celiac travelers (GF bread toasted and handled appropriately). Convenient cafeteria-style, no reservation needed.
- **Bright Angel Restaurant** ⚠️ — GF buns available for burgers, but fries are breaded and the fryer is shared. Order carefully: grilled items only, no fried anything.
- **Desert View Watchtower snack bar** ⚠️ — limited selection, cross-contamination risk. Van snacks are safer here.
- **Strategy:** Eat a real meal at El Tovar (reserve ahead), use Yavapai for convenience meals, cook from the van at Desert View.

### Evening: Hermit Road Sunset
Take the free park shuttle (red line) west from the Village to **Hopi Point** — widely considered the best sunset viewpoint on the South Rim. The canyon glows orange and red as the light drops. Shuttle runs until after sunset. Allow 2+ hours round trip.

## Day 14 — Apr 19: Morning + Drive to Zion

### Morning: Bright Angel Trail
A gentler, shadier morning hike before hitting the road:
- **Bright Angel Trail to 1.5-Mile Resthouse** — 3 miles round trip, more shade than South Kaibab, rest house with water (when operational) and a picnic area at the turnaround.
- A completely different feel from South Kaibab — canyon walls close in around you as you descend. Water/restrooms at the trailhead.

### Drive to Zion National Park
**~2.5 hours, 175 miles via US-89 North**

Leave Grand Canyon's East Entrance (convenient from Desert View), pick up US-89 north through the Navajo lands and the spectacular **Echo Cliffs** — red rock formations that build anticipation for Utah.

Notable stop: **Marble Canyon / Navajo Bridge** — two bridges side by side spanning the Colorado River gorge, 470 feet above the water. California condors often perch on the cliffs nearby. Free to stop, 10 minutes.

Continue north on US-89 through Kanab, UT — **better Celiac dining than you'd expect for a town this size** — then west on UT-9 into Zion Canyon.

**Celiac dining in Kanab:**
- **Rocking V Cafe** ✅ CELIAC-AWARE — 97 W Center St, (435) 644-8001. The best full-service Celiac option in Kanab. New American, menu clearly marks GF items including desserts. Staff trained on Celiac, will do what they can to avoid cross-contamination. Multiple positive Celiac reports. Reserve ahead — it fills up.
- **Wild Thyme Cafe** ✅ CELIAC-AWARE — healthy café with multiple GF options. A celiac diner ate there two nights in a row without issue.
- **Nany's Tacos** (if driving through from Williams earlier and not stopping there) — also has a presence/similar concept.
- ⚠️ **Three Bears** — was a Celiac-friendly option but **permanently closed as of early 2026.** Skip.

## Quick Tips

- **America the Beautiful Pass** — buy before entering Grand Canyon; covers all upcoming Utah parks too.
- **Cell service** is spotty inside the park. Download offline maps (Maps.me or Google offline) before arriving.
- **Free shuttles** run multiple routes through the park during busy season — use them instead of driving between viewpoints.
- **Kanab, UT** is a useful refuel/resupply point between Grand Canyon and Zion. Rocking V Cafe is the best Celiac-safe lunch stop on this stretch — worth building time around.
- **Grand Canyon dining:** Reserve El Tovar as far in advance as possible — it's the only truly reliable Celiac-safe option on the South Rim.
